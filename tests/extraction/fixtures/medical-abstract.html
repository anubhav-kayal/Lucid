Efficacy of mRNA-1273 Booster Vaccination in Immunocompromised Patients: A Phase 3 Randomized Controlled Trial

K. Yamamoto, MD, PhD, L. Hartmann, MD, R. Singh, MD, et al. · Affiliations: 12 institutions across 8 countries

Abstract

Background: Immunocompromised patients have reduced responses to standard mRNA vaccination regimens. The optimal booster schedule for this population remains unclear. We conducted a phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial to evaluate the efficacy and safety of an additional mRNA-1273 booster dose in immunocompromised adults aged 18 years or older.

Methods: Between January 15, 2023, and March 30, 2024, we enrolled 2,847 participants across 34 clinical sites in 12 countries. Participants were randomly assigned in a 2:1 ratio to receive either a 50-microgram booster dose of mRNA-1273 (1,898 participants) or a saline placebo (949 participants). The primary end point was the incidence of confirmed COVID-19 infection (any severity) through 6 months of follow-up. Secondary end points included severe disease, hospitalization, and death.

Results: The median age of participants was 56.4 years (interquartile range, 42.3 to 68.7 years). During the 6-month follow-up period, COVID-19 occurred in 84 of 1,898 participants (4.4%) in the booster group and 89 of 949 participants (9.4%) in the placebo group, representing a 57.2% relative risk reduction (95% confidence interval, 43.1 to 68.4; P < 0.001). Severe COVID-19 occurred in 12 participants in the booster group versus 31 in the placebo group (risk reduction, 79.8%). No vaccine-related serious adverse events were reported.

Conclusions: A 50-microgram booster dose of mRNA-1273 significantly reduced the risk of COVID-19 infection among immunocompromised adults, with no new safety concerns identified. These findings support the use of targeted booster vaccination strategies for this vulnerable population.
